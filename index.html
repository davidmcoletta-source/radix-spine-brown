Cervical · Upper-extremity pattern aid

Cervical disc level → exiting root.

In the cervical spine, disc-level naming maps differently than lumbar levels: a C5–C6 disc lesion most classically affects the C6 root, a C6–C7 lesion affects C7, and C7–T1 affects C8.

Select cervical disc level

Cervical naming rule

Cervical roots C1–C7 exit above their corresponding vertebrae, while C8 exits between C7 and T1. Clinically, subaxial disc lesions are usually described by the lower exiting root at that disc level.

Example A C6–C7 disc lesion most classically causes C7 radiculopathy.

Cervical clinical warning

  • C7 is often reported as the most frequently involved cervical nerve root, while C2–C4 radiculopathy is uncommon [Kang et al.].
  • Classic patterns are imperfect: McAnany et al. found standard textbook pain/numbness patterns in only 54% of 239 single-level surgical cases, with nonstandard patterns differing by an average of 1.68 dermatomal levels [McAnany et al.].
  • Screen for myelopathy when symptoms include hand clumsiness, gait imbalance, hyperreflexia, Babinski, clonus, Hoffmann sign, or bowel/bladder change; signs or symptoms of myelopathy warrant urgent referral [McCartney et al.].

Cervical root patterns synthesize Kang et al., McCartney et al., and the Merck Manual.

Thoracic · Chest and abdominal wall pattern aid

Thoracic level → band-like pattern.

Thoracic radiculopathy is usually a sensory-predominant syndrome: burning, shooting, or band-like pain wrapping from the back toward the chest or abdomen. Routine limb reflexes are usually less useful than the sensory stripe and myelopathy screen.

Select thoracic disc level

Thoracic pattern rule

Thoracic roots exit below their corresponding vertebrae and travel around the rib cage. A radicular presentation often feels like a unilateral band from the posterior thorax toward the anterior chest or abdominal wall.

Example T10 classically localizes near the umbilicus; T4 near the nipple line.

Thoracic clinical warning

  • Thoracic radiculopathy is the least common radiculopathy location, and symptoms often wrap around the front of the body in a dermatomal distribution [Johns Hopkins].
  • The most common presenting complaint is band-like chest wall pain; sensory loss may be asymmetric and dermatomal, while motor findings are often limited to intercostal or abdominal wall weakness [PM&R KnowledgeNow].
  • Thoracic and abdominal pain can mimic many visceral, rib, zoster, diabetic, infectious, or neoplastic conditions, and diagnosis may be delayed for months or years [Choi et al.].
  • Screen carefully for thoracic myelopathy. Bowel or bladder dysfunction, hyperreflexia, profound weakness, gait change, or a clear sensory level should prompt urgent evaluation [PM&R KnowledgeNow].

Thoracic mapping uses common dermatome landmarks and synthesis from PM&R KnowledgeNow, Johns Hopkins Medicine, and StatPearls — Spinal Nerves.

Lumbar/Sacral · Lower-extremity pattern aid

Lumbar disc level → nerve root deficit.

Select a disc level and herniation morphology. The tool returns the most likely affected root with its dermatome, myotome, reflex, and bedside clue.

Approximately 95% of lumbar disc herniations occur at L4–L5 or L5–S1 [StatPearls].

Select disc level

Herniation morphology

Why morphology matters

At a given lumbar level, the exiting root leaves through the neural foramen at that level, while the traversing root descends past the disc toward the foramen below. Paracentral herniations sit medial in the canal and compress the traversing root; far-lateral herniations sit out in the foramen and compress the exiting root [StatPearls].

Example A lateral L4–L5 lesion typically causes L5 radiculopathy; a far-lateral L4–L5 lesion typically causes L4 radiculopathy.

Clinical warning · Not a diagnosis

Read the output as a pattern, not a localizer.

  • Treat this tool as a pattern-recognition aid for trainees and clinicians, not a standalone localizer or substitute for examination, imaging, and clinical judgment.
  • Root functions overlap, particularly the L2–L4 group sharing anterior-thigh innervation, hip flexion, hip adduction, and knee extension. Muscle strength is often preserved because most muscles receive multi-root innervation [StatPearls — Radicular Back Pain].
  • Pain commonly does not follow textbook dermatomes. Murphy et al. found 64.1% of lumbar radicular pain patterns were non-dermatomal overall (L5 83.7%, L4 71.4%, L3 69.2%, L2 60.0%); only S1 was predominantly dermatomal at 64.9% [Murphy et al.].
  • Isolated sensory, motor, or reflex findings have imperfect accuracy. A BMC systematic review reported sensory testing sensitivity 0.61 / specificity 0.63, motor sensitivity 0.13–0.61, and reflex specificity up to 0.93 but sensitivity 0.14–0.67. The authors recommend consolidating findings rather than relying on a single test [Tawa et al.].
  • Disc morphology determines which root is compressed: paracentral and posterolateral lesions usually affect the traversing root, while far-lateral and extraforaminal lesions usually affect the exiting root [StatPearls].
  • Imaging is not diagnostic in isolation: roughly 27% of asymptomatic adults have disc herniation on MRI [StatPearls — Radicular Back Pain]. Correlate with history, exam, and red flags.
  • Urgent evaluation is warranted for progressive weakness, bowel or bladder dysfunction, saddle anesthesia, or any concern for cauda equina syndrome.

Please keep in mind

Nerves share a lot of the same skin and muscles, and real symptoms do not always follow the textbook. This is here to help you and your care team talk things through — it is not a diagnosis, and it does not replace a real exam.

  • Get medical care right away if you have new or fast-worsening weakness, trouble walking or keeping your balance, numbness in the groin or saddle area (where you sit), or any loss of control over your bladder or bowels.
  • If you feel symptoms on both sides of your body, or in several separate areas, the cause may be something other than a single pinched nerve.
